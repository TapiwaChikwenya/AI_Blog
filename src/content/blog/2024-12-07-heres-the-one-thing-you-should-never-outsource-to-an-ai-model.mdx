# Here’s the one thing you should never outsource to an AI model

<div class="text-sm text-gray-500 mb-4">
  Published on December 7, 2024 | Source: [VentureBeat AI](https://venturebeat.com/ai/heres-the-one-thing-you-should-never-outsource-to-an-ai-model/)
</div>


				<div>

<hr/>
</div><p>In a world where efficiency is king and disruption creates billion-dollar markets overnight, it&#x2019;s inevitable that businesses are eyeing <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/the-end-of-ai-scaling-may-not-be-nigh-heres-whats-next/">generative AI</a> as a powerful ally. From OpenAI&#x2019;s ChatGPT generating human-like text, to DALL-E producing art when prompted, we&#x2019;ve seen glimpses of a future where machines create alongside us &#x2014; or even lead the charge. Why not extend this into research and development (R&amp;D)? After all, AI could turbocharge idea generation, iterate faster than human researchers and potentially discover the &#x201c;next big thing&#x201d; with breathtaking ease, right?</p>



<p>Hold on. This all sounds great in theory, but let&#x2019;s get real: Betting on gen AI to take over your R&amp;D will likely backfire in significant, maybe even catastrophic, ways. Whether you&#x2019;re an early-stage startup chasing growth or an established player defending your turf, outsourcing <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/getting-started-with-ai-agents-part-2-autonomy-safeguards-and-pitfalls/">generative tasks</a> in your innovation pipeline is a dangerous game. In the rush to embrace new technologies, there&#x2019;s a looming risk of losing the very essence of what makes truly breakthrough innovations &#x2014; and, worse yet, sending your entire industry into a death spiral of homogenized, uninspired products.</p>



<div/><p>Let me break down why over-reliance on gen AI in R&amp;D could be innovation&#x2019;s Achilles&#x2019; heel.</p>



<h2>1. The unoriginal genius of AI: Prediction <strong>&#x2260;</strong> imagination</h2>



<p><a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/getting-started-with-ai-agents-part-1-capturing-processes-roles-and-connections/">Gen AI</a> is essentially a supercharged prediction machine. It creates by predicting what words, images, designs or code snippets fit best based on a vast history of precedents. As sleek and sophisticated as this may seem, let&#x2019;s be clear: AI is only as good as its dataset. It&#x2019;s not genuinely creative in the human sense of the word; it doesn&#x2019;t &#x201c;think&#x201d; in radical, disruptive ways. It&#x2019;s backward-looking &#x2014; always relying on what&#x2019;s already been created.</p>



<p>In R&amp;D, this becomes a fundamental flaw, not a feature. To truly break new ground, you need more than just incremental improvements extrapolated from historical data. Great innovations often arise from leaps, pivots, and re-imaginings, not from a slight variation on an existing theme. Consider how companies like Apple with the iPhone or Tesla in the electric vehicle space didn&#x2019;t just improve on existing products &#x2014; they flipped paradigms on their heads.</p>



<p>Gen AI might iterate design sketches of the next smartphone, but it won&#x2019;t conceptually liberate us from the smartphone itself. The bold, world-changing moments &#x2014; the ones that redefine markets, behaviors, even industries &#x2014; come from human imagination, not from probabilities calculated by an algorithm. When AI is driving your R&amp;D, you end up with better iterations of existing ideas, not the next category-defining breakthrough.</p>



<h2>2. Gen AI is a homogenizing force by nature</h2>



<p>One of the biggest dangers in letting AI take the reins of your product ideation process is that AI processes content &#x2014; be it designs, solutions or technical configurations &#x2014; in ways that lead to convergence rather than divergence. Given the overlapping bases of training data, AI-driven R&amp;D will result in homogenized products across the market. Yes, different flavors of the same concept, but still the same concept.</p>



<p>Imagine this: Four of your competitors implement <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/our-brains-are-vector-databases-heres-why-thats-helpful-when-using-ai/">gen AI systems</a> to design their phones&#x2019; user interfaces (UIs). Each system is trained on more or less the same corpus of information &#x2014; data scraped from the web about consumer preferences, existing designs, bestseller products and so on. What do all those AI systems produce? Variations of a similar result.</p>



<p>What you&#x2019;ll see develop over time is a disturbing visual and conceptual cohesion where rival products start mirroring one another. Sure, the icons might be slightly different, or the product features will differ at the margins, but substance, identity and uniqueness? Pretty soon, they evaporate.</p>



<p>We&#x2019;ve already seen early signs of this phenomenon in AI-generated art. In platforms like ArtStation, many artists have raised concerns regarding the influx of AI-produced content that, instead of showing unique human creativity, feels like recycled aesthetics remixing popular cultural references, broad visual tropes and styles. This is not the cutting-edge innovation you want powering your R&amp;D engine.</p>



<p>If every company runs gen AI as its de facto innovation strategy, then your industry won&#x2019;t get five or ten disruptive new products each year &#x2014; it&#x2019;ll get five or ten dressed-up clones.</p>



<h2>3. The magic of human mischief: How accidents and ambiguity propel innovation</h2>



<p>We&#x2019;ve all read the history books: Penicillin was discovered by accident after Alexander Fleming left some bacteria cultures uncovered. The microwave oven was born when engineer Percy Spencer accidentally melted a chocolate bar by standing too close to a radar device. Oh, and the Post-it note? Another happy accident &#x2014; a failed attempt at creating a super-strong adhesive.</p>



<p>In fact, failure and accidental discoveries are intrinsic components of R&amp;D. Human researchers, uniquely attuned to the value hidden in failure, are often able to see the unexpected as opportunity. Serendipity, intuition, gut feeling &#x2014; these are as pivotal to successful innovation as any carefully laid-out roadmap.</p>



<p>But here&#x2019;s the crux of the problem with <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/agi-is-coming-faster-than-we-think-we-must-get-ready-now/">gen AI</a>: It has no concept of ambiguity, let alone the flexibility to interpret failure as an asset. The AI&#x2019;s programming teaches it to avoid mistakes, optimize for accuracy and resolve data ambiguities. That&#x2019;s great if you&#x2019;re streamlining logistics or increasing factory throughput, but it&#x2019;s terrible for breakthrough exploration.</p>



<p>By eliminating the possibility of productive ambiguity &#x2014; interpreting accidents, pushing against flawed designs &#x2014; AI flattens potential pathways toward innovation. Humans embrace complexity and know how to let things breathe when an unexpected output presents itself. AI, meanwhile, will double down on certainty, mainstreaming the middle-of-road ideas and sidelining anything that looks irregular or untested.</p>



<h2>4. AI lacks empathy and vision &#x2014; two intangibles that make products revolutionary</h2>



<p>Here&#x2019;s the thing: Innovation is not just a product of logic; it&#x2019;s a product of empathy, intuition, desire, and vision. Humans innovate because they care, not just about logical efficiency or bottom lines, but about responding to nuanced human needs and emotions. We dream of making things faster, safer, more delightful, because at a fundamental level, we understand the human experience.</p>



<p>Think about the genius behind the first iPod or the minimalist interface design of Google Search. It wasn&#x2019;t purely technical merit that made these game-changers successful &#x2014; it was the empathy to understand user frustration with complex MP3 players or cluttered search engines. <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/here-are-3-critical-llm-compression-strategies-to-supercharge-ai-performance/">Gen AI</a> cannot replicate this. It doesn&#x2019;t know what it feels like to wrestle with a buggy app, to marvel at a sleek design, or to experience frustration from an unmet need. When AI &#x201c;innovates,&#x201d; it does so without emotional context. This lack of vision reduces its ability to craft points of view that resonate with actual human beings. Even worse, without empathy, AI may generate products that are technically impressive but feel soulless, sterile and transactional &#x2014; devoid of humanity. In R&amp;D, that&#x2019;s an innovation killer.</p>



<h2>5. Too much dependence on AI risks de-skilling human talent</h2>



<p>Here&#x2019;s a final, chilling thought for our shiny AI-future fanatics. What happens when you <a href="https://venturebeat.com/ai/enter-the-whisperverse-how-ai-voice-agents-will-guide-us-through-our-days/">let AI do too much</a>? In any field where automation erodes human engagement, skills degrade over time. Just look at industries where early automation was introduced: Employees lose touch with the &#x201c;why&#x201d; of things because they aren&#x2019;t flexing their problem-solving muscles regularly.</p>



<p>In an R&amp;D-heavy environment, this creates a genuine threat to the human capital that shapes long-term innovation culture. If research teams become mere overseers to AI-generated work, they may lose the capability to challenge, out-think or transcend the AI&#x2019;s output. The less you practice innovation, the less you become capable of innovation on your own. By the time you realize you&#x2019;ve overshot the balance, it may be too late.</p>



<p>This erosion of human skill is dangerous when markets shift dramatically, and no amount of AI can lead you through the fog of uncertainty. Disruptive times require humans to break outside conventional frames &#x2014; something AI will never be good at.</p>



<h2>The way forward: AI as a supplement, not a substitute</h2>



<p>To be clear, I&#x2019;m not saying gen AI has no place in R&amp;D &#x2014; it absolutely does. As a complementary tool, AI can empower researchers and designers to test hypotheses quickly, iterate through creative ideas, and refine details faster than ever before. Used properly, it can enhance productivity without squashing creativity.</p>



<p>The trick is this: We must ensure that AI acts as a supplement, not a substitute, to human creativity. Human researchers need to stay at the center of the innovation process, using AI tools to enrich their efforts &#x2014; but never abdicating control of creativity, vision or strategic direction to an algorithm.</p>



<p>Gen AI has arrived, but so too has the continued need for that rare, powerful spark of human curiosity and audacity &#x2014; the kind that can never be reduced to a machine-learning model. Let&#x2019;s not lose sight of that.</p>
